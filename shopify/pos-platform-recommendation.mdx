## The question being answered

Sixthgear's online store runs on a Next.js storefront built against the Shopify Storefront API. The business is evaluating whether to bring in-person point-of-sale (POS) onto **Loyverse** or **Shopify POS**, and specifically whether Loyverse could serve as the backend commerce engine for the whole business (online \+ in-store), or only as a standalone POS.

## Can Loyverse be a backend commerce engine?

**Short answer: no, not on its own.** Loyverse is built as a point-of-sale and inventory management system, not a headless commerce platform. It has a REST API, but that API is designed for syncing sales, inventory, and customer data with other systems — it does not include a storefront, cart, or checkout layer that a website can be built against the way our storefront is built against Shopify's Storefront API.

To run an online store with Loyverse as the backend, Loyverse itself has to be paired with a separate storefront platform (Shopify, WooCommerce, BigCommerce, etc.) and a third-party middleware connector (examples: Kosmos eSync, Octopus, SKUPlugs) that syncs products, inventory, and orders between the two. Loyverse becomes the POS and inventory system of record; the actual online store, cart, and checkout still live somewhere else.

In other words: **Loyverse is POS-and-inventory-first.** It is not a replacement for the Shopify Storefront API that our website is currently built on.

## Shopify POS vs. Loyverse

|  | Shopify POS | Loyverse |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Core purpose | In-person POS, natively unified with the same Shopify backend that powers our online store | In-person POS and inventory management, standalone |
| Online store | Included — same platform our storefront already uses | Not included — needs a separate platform plus middleware to sell online |
| Inventory sync with our site | Native, real-time, single source of truth (same Shopify Admin API our Next.js app already reads from) | Requires a third-party sync app; adds a moving part and potential lag/failure point |
| Pricing | POS Lite is free with any Shopify plan; POS Pro is around \$89/month per location for advanced features | Free core plan; paid add-ons (employee management, advanced inventory) around \$25–29/month per store |
| Ease of setup/use | Straightforward, but assumes you're already on Shopify | Very quick to set up; frequently cited as the easiest POS to learn |
| Best fit | Retailers who already sell online through Shopify and want one unified system | Very small, in-person-first businesses (single counter, no online store, or online store is an afterthought) |
| Limitations | Pro tier cost per location adds up across many stores; iOS-only devices | Lacks advanced reporting/warehouse features at scale; online selling always depends on a third-party bridge |

### Loyverse — pros

- Free core POS, low-cost add-ons
- Very easy to set up and train staff on
- Solid basics: inventory, employee management, loyalty, offline sales mode
- Has a REST API and app marketplace for connecting to accounting, ecommerce, and marketing tools

### Loyverse — cons

- No native online store or checkout — always requires bridging to a separate ecommerce platform through paid middleware
- Selling online through Loyverse means introducing a sync layer (extra cost, extra point of failure, sync delay risk) between POS and the actual storefront
- Would sit alongside our Shopify Storefront API integration rather than replace it — doesn't reduce our stack, it adds to it
- Lacks advanced features for scale (warehouse management, deep reporting)

### Shopify POS — pros

- Native, real-time sync with the exact same Shopify backend our Next.js storefront already reads from — one source of truth for products, inventory, and orders
- No middleware or third-party sync required to unify online and in-store selling
- POS Lite is free; included with any Shopify plan we're likely already paying for
- Built-in omnichannel features (buy-online-pickup-in-store, local delivery, unified customer profiles) that work directly with our existing storefront

### Shopify POS — cons

- POS Pro's per-location cost can add up for many physical locations
- iOS-only hardware requirement (no native Android support)
- Feature gaps for specialized retail/restaurant workflows may need extra apps

## Recommendation

Given that Sixthgear's online store is already built on Shopify's Storefront API, **Shopify POS is the stronger fit**. It shares the same backend our website already integrates with, so in-store and online inventory, orders, and customers stay unified without adding a middleware layer. Migrating to Loyverse would not replace or simplify our Shopify integration — since Loyverse isn't a headless commerce engine, our Storefront API-based website would still need Shopify (or another full ecommerce platform) behind it. Loyverse would only be added on the physical POS side, connected back to Shopify through a paid third-party sync app, which introduces cost and complexity without removing anything from our current stack.

Loyverse is a strong, affordable POS on its own — for a business that sells in person only, or treats online as secondary, it's a great choice. That isn't Sixthgear's situation: online is the primary channel and the storefront is already invested in Shopify's platform.
